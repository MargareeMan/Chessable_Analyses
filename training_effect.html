Estimating Training Effects in Chess Ratings Using Counterfactual Models

Author

George Ellis

1 Overview

This study investigates whether a training regimen produces measurable improvements in chess skill, using rating data inspired by the Lichess database.

We develop a sequence of Bayesian models, culminating in a mean-reverting latent skill model with explicit counterfactual construction.

2 Motivation

Online platforms match players by strength, which stabilizes win rates even when skill improves.

Therefore:

  • win rate is a poor indicator of improvement
  • rating trajectories must be modeled directly
  • causal attribution requires a counterfactual

3 Empirical Calibration

From matched players between January 2016 and January 2026:

  • Mean Elo change: 25.8
  • SD: 127.4
  • Strong mobility across rating bins

This implies:

  • weak long-run drift
  • high stochastic variability
  • likely mean reversion

4 Modeling Progression

4.1 v2.1: Random-Walk State Space

  • Failed to recover treatment effect
  • Counterfactual tracked factual path

4.2 v2.2: Piecewise Trend + AR(1)

  • Improved identification
  • Reduced confounding

4.3 v2.3: Mean-Reverting Latent Skill Model

  • Introduced mean reversion
  • Produced stable counterfactual
  • Recovered upward treatment effect

5 Model Specification (v2.3)

Latent skill evolves as:

\[ s_t = s_{t-1} + \kappa(\mu_t - s_{t-1}) + \varepsilon_t \]

where:

\[ \mu_t = \alpha + \beta_{pre} t + \delta \cdot post_t + \beta_{train} g_t \]

Counterfactual:

\[ \mu_t^{cf} = \alpha + \beta_{pre} t \]

Treatment effect:

\[ \tau_t = s_t - s_t^{cf} \]

6.2 Mean-Reverting Latent Skill with Explicit Counterfactual

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

df = pd.read_csv("model_observation_fits_v2_3.csv")

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.scatter(df["game"], df["elo_obs"], alpha=0.35, s=18, label="Observed Elo")
plt.plot(df["game"], df["skill_mean"], label="Estimated Latent Skill")
plt.plot(df["game"], df["skill_cf_mean"], linestyle="--", label="Estimated Counterfactual")
plt.axvline(x=200.5, linestyle=":", label="Training Start")
plt.legend()
plt.title("Mean-Reverting Latent Skill with Explicit Counterfactual")
plt.xlabel("Game Number")
plt.ylabel("Elo")
plt.show()

7 Key Findings

The mean-reverting latent skill model produced more realistic counterfactual trajectories than the earlier random-walk models. In particular, the estimated treatment effect in v2.3 increased over time in a way that matched the simulated truth much more closely.

8 Implications

These results suggest that mean reversion is an important ingredient when modeling long-run chess rating dynamics. They also suggest that single-player causal inference remains noisy, even when the model structure is improved.

9 Next Steps

Future work should extend the model to multiple players, longer post-training periods, and possibly explicit opponent-strength effects.
